Composter

    In Summer of 2018, I attended Summer Ventures in Science and Mathematics, a summer program hosted by the North Carolina School of Science and Math. I was among 60 students from across the state at Appalachian State University where I was able to work alongside university instructors to create a personal research project. Working with my partner Kate VanHorn, we found a problem where compost has to be constantly aerated in order for organic matter to compost correctly. After doing extensive research, we had not found a solution to the issue and decided to design and engineer our own.

    Our solution is a composter bucket with an auger inside that would be used to churn and mix the compost inside. On the lid of the bucket is a temperature and humidity sensor. From that sensor, the readings would be read by an Arduino running an Arduino program that I wrote. That program has a state function that would step through a variety of states to control the behavior of the auger depending on the temperature and humidity measurements. Because temperature and humidity changes as compost develops, it is important to maintain the ideal conditions for compost which is exactly what the Arduino and program do.

    At the end of the program we presented our research and project to our peers and other instructors. This project could be useful for anyone ranging from a personal backyard to industrial size facilities depending on the scale of the self-aerating composter. As a result of our extensive research and well engineered prototype, we were awarded an honorable mention for the Catalyst Award and later presented our project at the North Carolina Science Fair and qualified for the State Science Fair.

Self-Aerating Composter Features

Features include:

  • Arduino program with state function
  • Working prototype created from scratch
  • Incorporated temperature and humidity probe
  • Research paper and presentation
