Air Quality Analysis - Carolina Data Challenge

  • GitHub
  • GitHub

I decided to take part in the 2020 Carolina Data Challenge with a team of some of my classmates and friends. I had never taken part in a data challenge before or a statistics focused competition so this was an entirely new experience for me. For this challenge, we were given datasets on the air quality in both Durham, North Carolina as well as San Francisco, California. From this data we worked to create an interesting analysis on the different types of pollutants, causes for increases or decreases in pollutant levels, and possible solutions to combat air pollution.

Throughout this competition, my team and I worked in R to conduct our analysis due to its power in data analysis as well as being able to easily showcase our findings through an html document. To start off, we created different plots that charted average pollutant levels for PM2.5, PM10, CO, O3, SO2, and NO2 pollutants. We then compared the pollutant levels to national standards to determine how good air quality for both Durham and San Francisco really is. Many of our findings matched data scientific data and analyses that were already well established but the most interesting thing that we discovered was an an increase in PM2.5 levels in San Francisco. PM2.5 is particulate matter that is 2.5 microns or less in diameter. Our analysis concluded that the increase in PM2.5 was likely due to wildfires in California and the surrounding areas. This would lead to large amounts of smoke, and therefore particulate matter, that would disperse across the state leading to increased pollutant levels.

Air Quality Analysis Features

Features include:

  • ggplot graphs including average pollutant levels over time
  • Comparison of pollutant levels to national standards
  • Analysis of potential causes for changes in pollutant levels
